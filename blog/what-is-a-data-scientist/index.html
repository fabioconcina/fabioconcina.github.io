What is a Data Scientist

March 03, 2019

Data Science has emerged as a new and interdisciplinary field, with no clear boundaries and very wide in scope. It is often said that is a rebranding of Statistics, but in reality it borrows many techniques from the fields of Computer Science and Engineering.
The rise of the discipline and its exponential growth in the industry has been possible because of more and cheaper computational resources, easier to use open source software and abundance of data, where collection is increasingly commoditized.
Machine Learning is often highly associated with the term Data Science, but in fact it's only one single approach for the modeling of a problem. There are many areas of Applied Mathematics and Statistics that could easily fit under the Data Science umbrella (Operations Research, Numerical Analysis and Probability, to name a few).

The job of a Data Scientist can mean very different things depending on the company and its declination of the role.
From what I've seen from many job postings, at the time of writing (March, 2019) these are some recurring roles:

  • Data Scientist / Data Analyst
  • Data Scientist, Analytics / Analytics Software Engineer
  • Machine Learning Engineer
  • Machine Learning Scientist / Research Scientist

In the first one (Data Scientist / Data Analyst), the company needs to extract information from data sources not readily available to business users (e.g. corporate databases, business users are usually non-technical), maybe in aggregated form or joining multiple sources. The results will then be used for presentations and decision making, but the analysis is fundamentally ad hoc for the requirements.
Traditionally, this job has been called Data Analyst, but it could differ in the amount of statistical modeling used to answer business questions.

The second one (Data Scientist, Analytics / Analytics Software Engineer) involves building Analytics as a software for production use. This is more akin to the field of Software Engineering, with all the tooling needed (version control, testing, releases, issue tracking). It involves data modeling (ER diagram), fetching data from APIs, building ETL pipelines, developing visualizations on top of processed data, engineer new KPIs with the support of functional experts for the particular business. It requires in fact a non trivial amount of work to go from raw data to KPIs ready to consume, and a continuous maintenance of data pipelines when the software is deployed in production.

The third one (Machine Learning Engineer) involves building software that involves ML code. This means having knowledge of how to take care of feeding ML algorithms with data, how to do Feature Engineering and how to evaluate and optimize a model.
There is no need to invent novel approaches but one should have the skills to apply the standard tooling available.
ML also adds a whole set of challenges which are partially new to the field of Software Engineering in terms of complexity of system maintenance (see, for example: Machine Learning: The High-Interest Credit Card of Technical Debt).
A more specialized version of this role is a Deep Learning Engineer. Deep Learning requires additional knowledge of specific frameworks and popular deep neural network architectures, and experience in the use of GPUs to speed up the training phase of such models.
In other cases the specialization may derive from the specific application if that has considerable breadth, such in the case of Computer Vision Engineer or NLP Engineer.

The fourth one (Machine Learning Scientist / Research Scientist) is more similar to work done in academia. It involves actual research and study of current literature, and publishing of papers in internationally recognized conferences.

On top of this, things could made more complex when the volume of data is too big to handle on a single machine and with traditional technologies. In this case the need to use so called "Big Data" frameworks requires some more specialized skillsets, traditionally employed in the figures of Data Engineers / Big Data Engineers. The amount of Engineering effort required to deploy and maintain a distributed infrastructure is in fact much higher than the one required in a traditional setting.
